second order ordinary differential equation


A second order ordinary differential equation  F⁢(x,y,d⁢yd⁢x,d2⁢yd⁢x2)=0  can often be written in the form

d2⁢yd⁢x2=f⁢(x,y,d⁢yd⁢x). (1)

By its general solution one means a function   x↦y=y⁢(x)  which is at least on an interval twice differentiable and satisfies

y′′⁢(x)≡f⁢(x,y⁢(x),y′⁢(x)).

By setting  d⁢yd⁢x:=z,  one has  d2⁢yd⁢x2=d⁢zd⁢x,  and the equation (1) reads  d⁢zd⁢x=f⁢(x,y,z).  It is easy to see that solving (1) is equivalent (http://planetmath.org/Equivalent3) with solving the system of simultaneous first order (http://planetmath.org/ODE) differential equations

{d⁢yd⁢x=z,d⁢zd⁢x=f⁢(x,y,z), (2)

the so-called normal system of (1).

The system (2) is a special case of the general normal system of second order, which has the form

{d⁢yd⁢x=φ⁢(x,y,z),d⁢zd⁢x=ψ⁢(x,y,z), (3)

where y and z are unknown functions of the variable x.  The existence theorem of the solution

{y=y⁢(x),z=z⁢(x) (4)

is as follows; cf. the Picard–Lindelöf theorem (http://planetmath.org/PicardsTheorem2).

Theorem.  If the functions φ and ψ are continuous and have continuous partial derivatives with respect to y and z in a neighbourhood of a point  (x0,y0,z0),  then the normal system (3) has one and (as long as |x-x0| does not exceed a certain ) only one solution (4) which satisfies the initial conditions   y⁢(x0)=y0,z⁢(x0)=z0.  The functions (4) are continuously differentiable in a neighbourhood of x0.
